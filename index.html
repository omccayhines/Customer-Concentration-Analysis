Key Pointers

Measure Concentration

Rank P14's customers by revenue and work out what share the largest few hold — the top customer, the top 5 (CR5), the top 10 (CR10).

2

Quantify It With HHI

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index sums every customer's squared revenue share into one number. Above ~0.25 signals a dangerously concentrated base.

3

Avoid Univariate Thinking

Profit margin alone is misleading. Always ask: "What else should I know?" Consider concentration, loyalty, churn, and acquisition cost.

High-Level Business Steps

1
Define the risk question
expand_more

Primary question: how concentrated is P14's (Dragon Egg Replica) customer base, and how exposed is the business if a key customer leaves?

Sub-questions to keep in mind:

  • What share of P14 revenue comes from the single largest customer? From the top 5?
  • How many customers would have to leave to materially hurt P14?
  • Is P14's strong margin actually safe, or is it resting on one account?

This exercise deliberately follows the profitability one: P14 looked like the best product on margin. The job now is to stress-test that conclusion.

2
Gather customer-level data
expand_more

You need the same revenue foundation as before, then the customer angle:

  • Fact_Sales — the transactions, filtered down to product P14.
  • Dim_Realm and Dim_FX — currency and rate, so revenue is in USD.
  • Dim_Customer — optional context (loyalty tier, acquisition channel) for interpreting who the big customers are.

Watch out: P14 is priced in Gold Dragons, not USD. Revenue must be FX-converted — skipping that understates every figure and breaks the comparison.

3
Calculate each customer's revenue and share
expand_more

Build USD revenue per transaction, then total it by customer for P14 only. For each customer add:

  • Revenue — their total P14 spend in USD.
  • Revenue share — their revenue as a percentage of all P14 revenue.
  • Rank — ordering customers largest to smallest.

Revenue share is the metric the whole concentration analysis is built on.

4
Measure concentration (Pareto and HHI)
expand_more

Turn the shares into concentration measures:

  • Concentration ratios — CR5 and CR10: the combined share of the top 5 and top 10 customers.
  • HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index) — the sum of every customer's squared share. Above ~0.25 signals a highly concentrated, high-risk base.

This is the move from one-dimensional thinking (margin alone) to assessing the risk sitting behind that margin.

5
Add business context to the numbers
expand_more

Concentration figures describe the risk; they don't explain it. Layer in what you know about the customers:

  • Loyalty tier — is the dominant customer a sticky Gold/Platinum account or a Bronze one that could churn easily?
  • Acquisition channel — referral customers tend to retain better than walk-ins.

A 70% customer who is loyal and well-embedded is a very different risk from a 70% customer who could leave next quarter.

6
Quantify the profit at risk
expand_more

Translate concentration into money by running loss scenarios at P14's ~62% margin:

  • Lose the top customer — how much P14 profit disappears?
  • Lose the top 3, then the top 5 — how fast does the damage compound?

Scenarios make the risk concrete for decision-makers in a way an index value never does.

7
Develop the recommendation
expand_more

Tie the recommendation to the concentration level you found:

  • Low concentration — the base is diversified; support growth.
  • Moderate concentration — invest, but put retention programmes around the top customers.
  • High concentration — treat the dependency as a strategic risk: protect the key account hard while actively diversifying. In an M&A context, this is the kind of finding that justifies a valuation discount.

The headline: a high margin built on one customer is not the safe bet it first appears to be.

Power Query Quick Reference

Power Query Implementation Steps
expand_more

This guide builds the Customer Concentration query for product P14 (Dragon Egg Replica). Steps 1–4 are the same revenue build as the profitability exercise — revenue must be in USD before you can compare customers fairly. Steps 5–7 isolate P14 and measure how concentrated its customer base is.

warning

Common mistake: calculating revenue as UnitPriceLocal × Quantity with no FX conversion. P14 is a Game of Thrones product priced in Gold Dragons (rate 2), so skipping the conversion halves every figure. Always convert to USD first.

Step 1 — Load Fact_Sales into Power Query

Click path: Select any cell in Fact_Sales → Data → From Table/Range.

Why: Fact_Sales holds every transaction line. Customer-level revenue is summed up from here.

Step 2 — Bring in each realm's currency

Click path: Home → Merge Queries. Match RealmID to Dim_Realm's RealmID, Left Outer. Expand the new column, tick only CurrencyCode.

Why: The realm determines which currency a sale was made in — you need that before you can find the right exchange rate.

Step 3 — Bring in the FX rate

Click path: Merge Queries again. Match Dim_Realm.CurrencyCode to Dim_FX's CurrencyCode, Left Outer. Expand, tick only RateToReportingCurrency.

Why: This rate converts local prices to USD so every customer is measured in the same units.

Step 4 — Calculate revenue per transaction (USD)

Click path: Add Column → Custom Column. Name it Revenue USD.

[Quantity] * [UnitPriceLocal] * [Dim_FX.RateToReportingCurrency]

Why: Units × price × rate gives USD revenue for that line. This is the figure you'll total per customer.

Step 5 — Total revenue for each customer–product pair

Click path: Home → Group By. Group by two columns: CustomerID and ProductID (use the "Advanced" option to add the second). Aggregation: Revenue = Sum of Revenue USD.

Why: Grouping by customer and product gives one revenue figure per customer per product — the grain you need before isolating P14.

Step 6 — Keep only P14

Click path: Click the ProductID column dropdown → untick everything except P14 → OK. Then click the Revenue dropdown → Sort Descending.

Why: The question is only about P14's customers. Filtering now leaves 27 rows, one per P14 customer, biggest spender first.

Step 7 — Add each customer's share of P14 revenue

Click path: Add Column → Custom Column, name it Revenue as % total. Then set the column's data type to Percentage.

[Revenue] / List.Sum(#"Sorted Rows"[Revenue])

Note: #"Sorted Rows" is the name of the previous step — if your step is named differently, match it. List.Sum(...[Revenue]) totals the whole Revenue column.

Why: Dividing one customer's revenue by the P14 total gives their concentration share. This column is what the HHI and the top-5 analysis are built on.

Step 8 — Close & Load, then read the concentration story

Click path: Home → Close & Load.

Why: With shares in front of you, the risk is obvious: one customer (C077) is 69.5% of P14. HHI = Σ(share²) ≈ 0.49, far above the 0.25 high-risk line.

check_circle

Check your result: 27 customer rows, total Revenue $43,800.38. C077 ranked 1 at $30,445 (69.5%). Top 5 ≈ 78.4%, top 10 ≈ 86.6%, HHI ≈ 0.49.

Key Insights to Look For

Analysis Dimensions
expand_more

Concentration Findings:

  • Top Customer Dependency: Examine what % of P14 revenue comes from the largest customer
  • Top 5 Concentration (CR5): Calculate what % of total revenue comes from top 5 customers
  • Top 10 Concentration (CR10): Calculate what % of total revenue comes from top 10 customers
  • Pareto Analysis: 6 customers drive ~80% of P14 revenue
  • HHI Score: Calculate HHI to quantify concentration (benchmark: <0.15 = low, 0.15-0.25 = moderate, >0.25 = high)

Risk Assessment Insights - CATASTROPHIC RISK:

  • Profit Impact - Lose C077 (Top Customer): $30,444.88 revenue × 62.1% margin = $18,900 profit loss (69.5% of total P14 profit!)
  • Profit Impact - Lose Top 3 Customers: ~$32,618 revenue × 62.1% margin = ~$20,248 profit loss (74.5% of total P14 profit)
  • Profit Impact - Lose Top 5 Customers: ~$34,344 revenue × 62% margin = ~$21,320 profit loss (78.4% of total P14 profit)
  • Customer Loyalty: Examine if top customers are Gold/Platinum tier (sticky) or Bronze tier (at-risk)
  • Acquisition Channel: Check if top customers came via referral (higher retention) or walk-in (lower retention)
  • Transaction Frequency: Are top customers repeat buyers or one-time purchasers?

Univariate vs. Multivariate Analysis - The Critical Difference:

  • Univariate Analysis (Incomplete): "P14 has 62% margin and $27K profit → Let's invest heavily!"
  • Multivariate Analysis (Complete): "P14 has 62% margin and $27K profit, AND [your concentration findings] → This tells us [your strategic recommendation]"
  • The Key Question: What does your concentration analysis reveal about P14's true risk profile?
  • The Lesson: Always ask "What else should I know?" before making strategic decisions based on a single metric

Strategic Implications:

  • Customer Retention: What customer retention strategies should we implement based on your findings?
  • Diversification Strategy: If concentration is high, what's our plan to reduce dependency?
  • Pricing Strategy: How should concentration risk influence our pricing decisions?

Common Pitfalls

Pitfall Why It Happens How to Avoid
Assuming Profitability = Sustainability Focusing only on profit margin without considering customer concentration risk Always analyze customer concentration alongside profitability metrics; ask "What if we lose top customers?"
The Univariate Analysis Trap Examining one variable (profit) in isolation without multivariate context (concentration, loyalty, churn) Use the "What else should I know?" framework; consider profit + concentration + loyalty + acquisition cost simultaneously
Ignoring Customer Quality Treating all customers equally without considering loyalty tier, acquisition channel, or transaction frequency Weight concentration risk by customer quality; Gold tier referral customers are stickier than Bronze walk-ins
Misinterpreting HHI Not understanding HHI scale or thresholds (0-1 range, where >0.25 = high concentration) Learn HHI benchmarks: <0.15 = Low | 0.15-0.25 = Moderate | >0.25 = High concentration
Forgetting Profit Impact Scenarios Calculating concentration % without translating to actual profit loss scenarios Always calculate: Revenue loss × Profit margin = Profit impact for top 1, 3, 5, and 10 customers
Not Considering Acquisition Cost Focusing only on current revenue without factoring in customer acquisition cost (CAC) or lifetime value (LTV) Combine concentration analysis with CAC/LTV; a high-revenue customer with high CAC may be less valuable

Pro Tips

Advanced Analysis Techniques
expand_more

M&A Due Diligence Application:

  • Valuation Impact: Customer concentration >25% (CR5) often triggers 10-30% valuation discounts
  • Due Diligence Checklist: Calculate HHI, CR5, CR10 for target company's customer base
  • Negotiation Strategy: Use concentration findings to negotiate earn-outs tied to customer retention
  • Integration Planning: High concentration = prioritize customer retention in first 100 days post-acquisition

Supplier Concentration Analysis:

  • Apply same methodology to supplier base (operational risk instead of revenue risk)
  • High supplier concentration = supply chain vulnerability and negotiation disadvantage
  • Calculate HHI for suppliers; if >0.25, develop supplier diversification strategy
  • Combine with supplier criticality assessment (concentration + single-source components = highest risk)

Customer Profitability Matrix (2×2 Framework):

  • Stars (High Concentration + High Profitability): VIP retention programs, dedicated account managers
  • Cash Cows (High Concentration + Low Profitability): Renegotiate pricing or consider exit
  • Growth Opportunities (Low Concentration + High Profitability): Expand customer base in this segment
  • Dogs (Low Concentration + Low Profitability): Deprioritize or discontinue

Monitoring and Early Warning Systems:

  • Quarterly HHI Tracking: Set up automated dashboards to monitor concentration trends
  • Alert Thresholds: Trigger alerts when HHI increases by >0.05 or any customer exceeds 10% of revenue
  • Churn Prediction: Monitor transaction frequency, support tickets, payment delays for top customers
  • Proactive Retention: Implement retention campaigns before customers show churn signals

Geographic and Product Concentration:

  • Extend analysis to geographic markets (revenue concentration by region/country)
  • Analyze product concentration (% of revenue from top 5 products)
  • Calculate multi-dimensional HHI (customer × geography × product)
  • Diversification strategies should address all concentration dimensions simultaneously

Real-World Example - Tech Startup Valuation:

  • Scenario: SaaS startup with $10M ARR, 70% gross margin, seeking Series B funding
  • Initial Valuation: $50M (5x revenue multiple)
  • Due Diligence Finding: Top 3 customers = 45% of ARR (HHI = 0.28 - high concentration)
  • Investor Response: Valuation reduced to $35M (3.5x revenue) due to concentration risk
  • Outcome: Startup implemented customer diversification strategy, reduced HHI to 0.12 over 18 months
  • Series C Result: Raised at 6x revenue ($60M valuation) with improved concentration profile
  • Lesson: Customer concentration directly impacts valuation - diversification creates shareholder value

LTV-Weighted HHI (Advanced Metric):

  • Concept: Weight concentration by customer lifetime value (LTV) instead of current revenue
  • Formula: LTV-HHI = Σ(LTV Share)² for all customers
  • Why It Matters: High-concentration customers with high LTV are less risky than those with low LTV
  • Application: Adjust concentration risk assessments based on customer stickiness and retention rates
